The Cost of Fertiliser

Global fertiliser prices are rising again. Here's why it matters.

Global fertiliser prices have begun rising again as conflict involving Iran threatens production and exports from the Middle East. It echoes what happened after Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022, when fertiliser and energy markets were thrown into turmoil. But this time the risk is different: the Middle East sits at the heart of global fertiliser supply.

The region is both a major producer and a crucial transit hub. Around 35% of globally traded urea passes through the Strait of Hormuz, one of the world’s most important shipping routes. Urea is the most widely used nitrogen fertiliser and is what keeps a huge share of the world's crops growing. Any disruption to production in the Gulf - or to shipping through the strait - can quickly tighten supply for farmers around the world.

The timing feels familiar. Russia's invasion of Ukraine began in February 2022, just as farmers across the northern hemisphere were preparing for the planting season. The same is happening now. As farmers start buying fertiliser for the coming season, rising prices and potential shortages could start affecting what they actually decide to plant.

But the impact might play out differently this time. Ukraine's invasion immediately disrupted grain exports, sending food prices sharply higher. Fertiliser shocks work more slowly: they raise the cost of growing crops rather than removing food from markets overnight. But if farmers can't afford fertiliser or can't access it, yields could fall across multiple regions later in the year.
