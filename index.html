The Cost of Fertilizer

Global fertiliser prices are rising again. Here's why it matters.

Global fertiliser prices have begun rising again as conflict involving Iran threatens production and exports from the Middle East. The surge echoes the shock that followed Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022, when fertiliser and energy markets were thrown into turmoil. But this time the risk runs through a different channel: the Middle East sits at the heart of global fertiliser supply.

The region is both a major producer and a crucial transit hub. Around 35% of globally traded urea passes through the Strait of Hormuz, one of the world’s most important shipping routes. Urea is the most widely used nitrogen fertiliser and underpins a large share of global crop production. Any disruption to production in the Gulf — or to shipping through the strait - can quickly tighten supply for farmers around the world.

The timing is strikingly similar to the Ukraine shock. Russia’s invasion began in February 2022, just as farmers across the northern hemisphere were preparing for the planting season. The current escalation is unfolding at almost the same point in the agricultural calendar. As farmers begin purchasing fertiliser for the coming growing season, rising prices and potential shortages could quickly feed into planting decisions.

The impact, however, may unfold differently. Ukraine's invasion immediately disrupted grain exports, sending food prices sharply higher. Fertiliser shocks work more slowly: they raise the cost of growing crops rather than removing food from markets overnight. But if farmers cannot afford fertiliser or cannot access it, yields could fall across multiple regions later in the year.
