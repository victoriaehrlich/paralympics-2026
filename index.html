About this week

This week I'm learning pie charts in D3.js. Pie charts get a bad reputation in data visualisation circles - you'll find plenty of strongly-worded Medium posts about how humans are terrible at reading angles - but I think there's a time and a place for them, and that time is when you have a simple part-to-whole story with a small number of categories. This week's topic gave me exactly that.

A note on the third chart: I almost put disabled girls and boys into a single pie chart. Those two percentages don't add up to 100% - they're two separate statistics measuring the same thing in two different groups. Putting them in one pie would imply they're slices of a whole, which is misleading. Two donuts, one per group, is the honest version. Sometimes the right chart is the one that doesn't lie.

Here's my random Friday afternoon thoughts on data and whatever chart I'm currently trying to build.

Have a nice weekend!

Not Equal Yet: Women at the Winter Paralympics

The Milano Cortina 2026 Paralympics just wrapped up. The headline was progress. The detail tells a more complicated story.

The Milano Cortina 2026 Paralympic Winter Games closed this week — and the official narrative has been upbeat. It was billed as the most gender-balanced edition in Winter Games history, with a record number of female athletes and new women's events. A total of 160 women competed across six sports, an 18% increase over the previous record of 136 women at Beijing 2022.

That's real progress. And it also isn't the full picture.

611 athletes from 55 nations competed at Milano Cortina 2026. Of those, 160 were women. That still means nearly three quarters of the field were men — and compared to the Summer Games, the gap is stark. At Paris 2024, women made up 44.7% of athletes. At Milano Cortina, 26.2%. The IPC has always indicated a desire for gender parity. The direction of travel in winter sport is slower.

Athletes at Milano Cortina 2026, by gender

Data source: International Paralympic Committee, Milano Cortina 2026

The gap is partly structural — and nowhere is that clearer than in para ice hockey. The sport is officially mixed-gender: there are no separate men's and women's events. In practice, Japan's Akari Fukunishi was the only woman among 135 players at these Games — the fourth woman to compete in the sport's entire Paralympic history.

This isn't because there are no women playing para ice hockey. Selection is country-led and quota-based — each nation picks its squad from a small fixed allocation, and there is no dedicated women's pathway at Paralympic level. For a standalone women's event to exist, the sport needs to demonstrate representation from at least eight national teams across three regions, plus multiple World Championships. Women's para ice hockey hasn't reached those benchmarks yet. A separate tournament is likely still several Games away — possibly 2030 or 2034.

Para snowboard tells a similar story. Men competed in six medal events at Milano Cortina. Women competed in two.

The broader numbers on aspiration make for uncomfortable reading. A 2025 report by Women in Sport and Activity Alliance surveyed young disabled people following the Paris 2024 Paralympics. The Games inspired 74% of them to do more sport — but who gets to dream of reaching the top is far from equal.

A lack of visibility is known to affect participation at all levels of sport, but it's especially important at the Paralympics, which may be the only global media moment a para athlete gets in four years. If disabled girls don't see people like them competing, the dream gap widens before it even has a chance to close.

Stephanie Hilborne OBE, CEO of Women in Sport, said: "This report reveals the extent to which disabled girls are being left behind in sport. While nearly two-thirds of disabled boys dream of reaching the top, only a third of disabled girls feel the same. That's not down to lack of ambition — it's the weight of gender stereotypes and the added barriers of disability, crushing opportunity and depriving young disabled women of joy. Sport has the power to transform lives, to bring self-belief and belonging. We must challenge the stereotypes and work to create more opportunities to create a future where every girl feels sport is a place she belongs."

The record this week was real. So is the distance still to go.
