About this week

This week I learnt all about donut charts. And I'll be honest, in my day-to-day work I'm not a fan and I never use them. There's a lot of controversy around them in the community, people trying to fill it too much and it becomes impossible to read: pie charts with eight slices and labels so small you need to squint. And I've always thought a bar chart almost always does the job better. But this week I'm learning them in D3.js, so I had to find a use case I could actually defend.

That meant looking for topics where a single data point tells the whole story - one number as a share of a whole. The Paralympics closing last weekend gave me tht opportunity.

That said, arcs and donuts have a lot of potential and some of (what I think) the most beautiful custom visuals in the industry are built on this foundation. One of my favourites is Nadieh Bremer's Olympic Feathers, which also explores Olympics data. It uses arcs and radians driven by medal counts, stacked and layered across time. This week I could start to understand parts - even if small parts - of her code and the different elements to it. So maybe donut charts aren't that bad after all.

Anyway, here’s my random Friday afternoon thoughts on data and whatever chart I’m currently trying to build.

Have a nice weekend!

Not Equal Yet: Women at the Winter Paralympics

The Milano Cortina 2026 Paralympics just wrapped up. The headline was progress. The detail tells a more complicated story.

The Milano Cortina 2026 Paralympic Winter Games closed this week and the official narrative has been very positive. It was the most gender-"balanced" edition in Winter Games history, with a record number of female athletes and new women's events. A total of 160 women competed across six sports, compared to the previous record of 136 women at the Beijing 2022 winter edition.

That is progress but there are also layers to that.

611 athletes from 55 nations competed at Milano Cortina 2026. And of those, 160 were women. That means nearly three quarters of the field were men - so while more women are progressing we're still far from being gender-balanced. Compared to the Summer Games, the difference is quite big: at the Paris 2024 edition, women made up 44.7% of athletes, which indeed is the better figure. The IPC has always indicated they were striving for gender parity, and there is an effort, but the Winter Games are moving more slowly. A big part of that comes down to how some sports are structured.

Data source: International Paralympic Committee, Milano Cortina 2026

Para ice hockey is a good example of what structural barriers mean at the Paralympics. The sport is officially mixed-gender and both women and men can compete together. This year Japan's Akari Fukunishi was the only woman among 135 players at these Games and the fourth woman to compete in the sport's entire Paralympic history.

But why was there only one woman competing? It is not because there are no women playing para ice hockey. Selection is country-led and quota-based: each nation picks its squad from a small fixed allocation, and there is no dedicated women's pathway at Paralympic level. For a standalone women's event to exist, the sport needs to show representation from at least eight national teams across three regions, plus multiple World Championships. Women's para ice hockey hasn't reached those benchmarks yet and a standalone women's event is still likely several Games away.

Representation matters in sport for the same reason it matters anywhere else - it shapes ambitions and dreams for those watching. The Paralympics are one of the biggest stages in the world for para athletes, and when so few women are competing, the absence is hard to ignore. A 2025 report by Women in Sport and Activity Alliance found that only 34% of disabled girls dream of reaching the top in sport, compared to 61% of disabled boys. If the athletes on that stage don't reflect the people watching, that gap is unlikely to close on its own.

Stephanie Hilborne OBE, CEO of Women in Sport, said: "This report reveals the extent to which disabled girls are being left behind in sport. While nearly two-thirds of disabled boys dream of reaching the top, only a third of disabled girls feel the same. That's not down to lack of ambition - it's the weight of gender stereotypes and the added barriers of disability, crushing opportunity and depriving young disabled women of joy. Sport has the power to transform lives, to bring self-belief and belonging. We must challenge the stereotypes and work to create more opportunities to create a future where every girl feels sport is a place she belongs."

The record this week was a move in the right direction, but there still is distance to go.
